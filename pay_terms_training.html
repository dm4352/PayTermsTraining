Introduction to Payment Terms

Payment terms define when and how payments should be made for goods or services. They impact cash flow and are critical for financial management.

Net 45 Days

What it means: Payment is due 45 calendar days after the invoice date.

Calculation: Simply add 45 calendar days to the invoice date to determine when payment is due.

Pros and Cons: This method provides customers with the exact same payment window for every invoice, regardless of when it's issued. However, it creates scattered payment dates throughout the month, requiring customers to process multiple payments on different days. For suppliers, this means a steady stream of payments rather than a concentrated influx at month-end.

Net 1st Month, EOM

What it means: Invoices issued any day in April are due May 31.

Calculation: All invoices issued during a calendar month become due on the last day of the following month.

Pros and Cons: This approach creates a single, consistent payment date for all invoices from a given month, allowing customers to batch payments into one transaction. Late-month invoices receive less time to pay than early-month ones, but the administrative efficiency of consolidated payments often outweighs this drawback. Suppliers benefit from predictable cash flow timing and simplified receivables management.

Net 2nd Month, EOM

(Note: This is a non-standard term used only used for a limited number of customers. Not part of our standard terms.)

What it means: Invoices in April are due June 30.

Calculation: All invoices from the current month become due on the last day of the second month following the invoice month.

Pros and Cons: This extends the payment window substantially while still maintaining the benefit of consolidated payments. Customers gain significant payment flexibility with a longer overall term, making it attractive for larger purchases. As with N1MthEOM, customers sacrifice equal payment periods in exchange for administrative efficiency of a single payment date, while suppliers must wait longer for payment but benefit from predictable receivables timing.

2% 10th Prox, Net EOM Prox

(Note: This is a non-standard term used only used for a limited number of customers. Not part of our standard terms.)

What it means:

  • 2% 10th Prox: 2% off if paid by 10th of following month.
  • Net EOM Prox: Full due by month-end if no discount.

Calculation: Two options - pay by the 10th of the following month for a 2% discount, or pay the full amount by the month's end.

Pros and Cons: This dual-option approach incentivizes faster payment while still offering flexibility. Customers can choose between saving money through prompt payment or maximizing their cash flow by paying later. For suppliers, it encourages faster receivables collection while still accommodating customers who need the full term. The discount creates a win-win situation where both parties can optimize based on their current financial priorities.
